TIPP

DBT — Distress Tolerance
What it helps

Acute nervous system dysregulation, crisis states, physiological threat response

Why it works

Linehan (1993): Four fastest physiological interventions for bringing the nervous system out of survival mode. Each component targets a different physiological mechanism.

How to do it

T: Splash cold water on face or hold ice. I: 20 minutes intense exercise. P: Inhale 4 counts, exhale 6 counts for 5 minutes. P: Tense and release major muscle groups progressively.

Half-Smiling and Willing Hands

DBT — Distress Tolerance
What it helps

Anger, resistance, tension held in the body

Why it works

Ekman (1993) facial feedback research: physical expression influences emotional state. Linehan applied to distress tolerance.

How to do it

Half-smile: slightly upturn corners of mouth. Relax face. Hold 30 seconds. Willing hands: palms up, fingers relaxed. Both signal acceptance to the nervous system.

Urge Surfing

DBT — Distress Tolerance
What it helps

Overwhelming urges, rumination cycles, compulsive behaviors

Why it works

Marlatt (mindfulness-based relapse prevention): Urges are waves. They build, peak, and fall. Observing without acting demonstrates this.

How to do it

Notice the urge arising. Rate its intensity 1-10. Observe it without acting. Watch it build and peak. Notice it begin to fall. Rate again. Most urges peak within 20-30 minutes.

Paced Breathing

Somatic
What it helps

Acute stress response, cortisol flooding, sympathetic nervous system activation

Why it works

Porges (polyvagal theory): The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve and shifts the nervous system from sympathetic to parasympathetic. This is physiology, not self-help.

How to do it

Inhale 4 counts. Exhale 6 counts. The exhale must be longer than the inhale. 5 minutes. Can be done anywhere. No equipment needed.

Diving reflex research: Cold water on the face triggers the mammalian diving reflex, rapidly slowing heart rate. Fastest physiological reset available.

Shaking and Tremoring

Somatic
What it helps

Opposite Action

DBT — Emotion Regulation
What it helps

Emotion-driven avoidance, shame-driven hiding, fear-driven withdrawal

Why it works

Linehan (1993): When the emotion urges one behavior, doing the opposite breaks the emotion-action cycle.

How to do it

Identify what the emotion is urging you to do. Ask: is acting on this effective? If not, do the opposite. Shame says hide: tell one safe person. Fear says avoid: approach for 5 minutes.

Movement

Somatic
What it helps

Cortisol accumulation, depression, low energy, trapped emotions

Why it works

Van der Kolk (The Body Keeps the Score): The body stores stress and trauma. Movement releases it. Any form of movement reduces cortisol independent of intensity.

How to do it

Any movement counts. Walk. Stretch. Dance alone in your home. Shake arms and legs for 1 minute. The nervous system does not require a gym.

Behavioral Activation

CBT
What it helps

Depression, withdrawal, low motivation, waiting to feel better before acting

Why it works

Lewinsohn (1974) / Martell: Structured activity reduces depression and anxiety independent of mood. Action changes mood. Mood does not have to change before action.

How to do it

Schedule one meaningful activity. Do it regardless of mood. Track mood before and after. The data shows action produces mood change, not the reverse.

Self-Compassion Practice

Positive Psychology
What it helps

CBT
What it helps

Anhedonia, loss of positive experience, inability to identify what helps

Why it works

Lewinsohn (1974): Tracking activities for pleasure and mastery builds awareness of what produces positive experience so it can be deliberately increased.

How to do it

Rate each activity daily: pleasure 0-10 and mastery 0-10. Review weekly. Increase activities that score high on either. This is data, not journaling.

Progressive Muscle Relaxation

Somatic
What it helps

Why it works

Jacobson (1938): Systematic tension and release of muscle groups reduces physical tension and activates the relaxation response.

Rejection Sensitivity Reframe

CBT / SFBT
What it helps

Anticipatory anxiety about rejection in job search, hypervigilance to social rejection signals

Why it works

Downey and Feldman (1996): Rejection sensitivity is a learned expectation, not an accurate prediction. Reframing past rejection as evidence of survival rather than evidence of inadequacy.

How to do it

Identify a past rejection or loss. Write: what did I survive? What did I learn? What do I know now that I did not know then? This is not toxic positivity. It is evidence of resilience.

Savoring

Positive Psychology
What it helps

Rushing past positive experiences, negativity bias dominating attention

Why it works

Bryant and Veroff (2007): Deliberately attending to and appreciating positive experiences counteracts the negativity bias and builds positive emotional memory.

How to do it

Identify one positive experience today, however small. Stop. Pay full attention to it. Describe it mentally in detail. Extend it as long as possible. The nervous system cannot distinguish between a savored small moment and a large event.

Self-Compassion Practice

Positive Psychology
What it helps

Self-criticism, shame, harsh inner critic, unrealistic standards under stress

Why it works

Neff (University of Texas): Self-compassion produces resilience. Self-criticism impairs recovery. Three components: acknowledge suffering, common humanity, self-kindness.

How to do it

Step 1: This is a moment of suffering. Step 2: Suffering is part of being human. Others feel this too. Step 3: May I be kind to myself right now. What would I say to a friend in this situation? Say that to yourself.

Your Steady Center

Wise Mind · DBT — Mindfulness
What it helps

Decisions made purely from emotion or purely from logic, missing the integration

Why it works

Linehan (1993): Wise mind is the intersection of emotional mind and rational mind. Both contain information. Neither alone is sufficient.
